# Top Risks Needed

## Top Risks

| Risk | Why It Matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| `Recommendation quality and clinician trust risk` | This is the biggest product risk. If the recommendation is incomplete, inaccurate, or not actionable, clinicians will ignore it just like many existing real-time benefit and alert tools. The product only works if clinicians trust that it helps them make a better decision in the room. | Start in narrow specialty use cases with high-friction drugs. Make outputs explainable, not black-box. Show the top drivers of risk and recommended alternatives with rationale. Measure acceptance rate, first-fill success, and time-to-therapy. |
| `Regulatory classification risk` | If the product is positioned as choosing therapy rather than informing access feasibility, it could face much heavier FDA scrutiny as clinical decision software. That would slow launch and raise cost materially. | Position it as an explainable clinician-facing access assistant, not an autonomous prescribing tool. Focus on access feasibility, not clinical superiority. Keep the clinician in control and require review before any action. |
| `Data quality and freshness risk` | Even a strong model will fail if payer rules, formulary status, routing constraints, or cost data are stale or incomplete. This can quickly undermine trust. | Show source freshness, disclose missing inputs, and limit claims when data confidence is low. Start with the highest-value data sources first rather than pretending full coverage. |
| `Workflow adoption risk` | If the tool adds clicks, interrupts the visit, or feels like another alert, clinicians will bypass it. | Fit into the prescribing moment, keep the output short and actionable, and support the patient conversation rather than creating extra admin work. |

## Recommended Top Two

1. `Recommendation quality and clinician trust risk`
2. `Regulatory classification risk`

## case study-Ready Framing

The two biggest risks are, first, `recommendation quality and clinician trust`, because this product will fail if it feels like another inaccurate alert rather than an actionable guide. Second, `regulatory classification risk`, because if the product is framed as choosing therapy rather than supporting access feasibility, it could trigger much heavier FDA scrutiny. To mitigate these, the product should remain explainable, narrowly focused on access execution, and visibly subordinate to clinician judgment.
