# Medication Access Mismatch - Customer Segment Recommendation

## Recommendation in one sentence

The best initial customer segment is **large U.S. health systems or IDNs with specialty-heavy ambulatory clinics, centralized medication access teams, and preferably an owned or affiliated specialty pharmacy**, starting with **rheumatology, gastroenterology, dermatology, and neurology** as the first specialty wedge.

## Why this is the best segment

The problem statement and research report both show that the medication access mismatch is real, but it is not evenly distributed across all outpatient prescribing. The pain is most acute where medications are expensive, prior authorization is common, formulary and step-therapy barriers are frequent, routing is complex, and staff already spend significant time coordinating starts.

This makes large specialty-heavy provider organizations the best first segment because they combine:

- high pain intensity
- enough workflow volume to justify buying software
- measurable financial and operational ROI
- existing staff who can act on recommendations
- a realistic technical environment for integration and deployment

## Primary target segment

### Organization profile

Target:

- large health systems
- integrated delivery networks
- large multispecialty medical groups with centralized medication access operations

Best-fit attributes:

- specialty-heavy ambulatory care footprint
- meaningful branded, specialty, or access-friction medication volume
- centralized or semi-centralized medication access, prior auth, or specialty pharmacy teams
- strong EHR footprint and governance
- executive interest in reducing therapy delays, staff burden, and patient leakage

### Best specialty wedge

Start with:

- rheumatology
- gastroenterology
- dermatology
- neurology

These specialties are the best opening wedge because they frequently involve biologics and specialty therapies with high access friction, but usually fit a more repeatable ambulatory workflow than the most operationally complex oncology pathways.

### Strong secondary wedges

Expand next into:

- endocrinology and obesity medicine
- pulmonology and allergy
- oral oncology programs

## Segment recommendation from three perspectives

### 1. Market researcher perspective

From a market attractiveness standpoint, this segment is the strongest because it sits at the center of the pain the research report describes: high-cost therapy starts, frequent payer restrictions, affordability shocks, and routing complexity.

Why it wins:

- The access problem is most severe in high-friction specialty prescribing, not in routine low-cost primary care prescribing.
- Large health systems and IDNs have enough prescriber volume and enough operational burden to create a meaningful software budget.
- They are more likely than small practices to feel the full economic impact of delayed starts, staff rework, pharmacy leakage, and patient dissatisfaction.
- The category is still fragmented. Existing tools solve pieces of the problem such as `ePA`, `RTBT`, affordability navigation, or specialty pharmacy onboarding, but not the full orchestration workflow.

Strategic implication:

The product should be positioned as **medication access orchestration** for specialty-heavy ambulatory care, not as another prior authorization tool.

### 2. Sales engineer perspective

From a technical pre-sales perspective, this segment is attractive because it supports a credible pilot and a believable implementation story.

Why it wins:

- Large provider organizations usually have a stable EHR environment, clearer IT governance, and existing digital prescribing workflows.
- Centralized medication access teams create a clear operational landing point for work queues, case tracking, and handoffs.
- Specialty-heavy ambulatory programs create enough high-friction cases to demonstrate value quickly in a proof of concept.
- If the organization has an owned or affiliated specialty pharmacy, the product can show value across routing, capture, time to first fill, and handoff quality.

What makes the pilot technically credible:

- a defined specialty or clinic scope
- access to prescribing and payer-related workflow data
- measurable baseline for time to therapy start and first-fill conversion
- identifiable integration and stakeholder owners

Technical risks to avoid in the first segment:

- small practices with limited IT support
- organizations without centralized access workflows
- highly fragmented multi-EHR environments
- customers expecting a fully custom workflow engine before value is proven

### 3. Customer success manager perspective

From a customer success standpoint, this segment gives the best chance of onboarding success, measurable adoption, and renewal.

Why it wins:

- There are clear daily users: medication access coordinators, prior auth staff, and prescribing clinicians in targeted specialties.
- Time-to-value can be demonstrated through visible operational metrics rather than vague satisfaction claims.
- The workflow is painful enough that teams are likely to adopt a product that removes manual rework and clarifies next steps.
- Cross-functional stakeholder value is easier to show because clinicians, staff, pharmacy, and leadership all benefit from faster and cleaner therapy starts.

Best customer success conditions:

- one executive sponsor
- one operational owner for medication access workflows
- one clinical champion in the specialty
- baseline and post-launch metrics tracked monthly
- a narrow first rollout with clear enablement and support

Renewal and expansion are most likely when the product proves:

- faster therapy starts
- fewer abandoned prescriptions
- lower manual rework
- better staff visibility into stuck cases
- clearer patient communication

## Ideal customer profile

The ideal first customer looks like this:

- U.S. provider organization with large ambulatory specialty footprint
- centralized medication access or prior auth operations
- high volume of specialty or branded prescriptions
- leadership already aware that current tools such as `ePA` and `RTBT` are not enough
- willingness to pilot in one or two specialties before scaling enterprise-wide
- ability to measure workflow, access, and patient-start outcomes

### Core users

- medication access coordinators
- prior authorization specialists

### Key gatekeepers

- IT and integration leads

- specialty pharmacy leadership when relevant

## Why not start elsewhere

### Do not start with small independent practices

They have pain, but usually lack the staff structure, buying capacity, and implementation resources to support a workflow platform sale and sustained adoption.

### Do not start with broad primary care

Primary care offers scale, but the medication access mismatch is less concentrated and less economically urgent on a per-site basis. This makes ROI harder to prove quickly.

### Do not start with the most complex enterprise-wide deployment

An all-specialty, all-clinic rollout increases implementation risk and slows time to proof. The first win should come from a focused specialty launch.

## Recommended entry strategy

### Phase 1: land

Sell into one large health system or IDN with:

- centralized medication access operations
- one shared EHR environment
- one to two target specialties
- clear executive sponsorship

### Phase 2: prove

Use the pilot to show:

- reduction in time to therapy start
- higher first-fill success
- fewer reroutes and re-prescribes
- lower staff follow-up burden
- improved patient expectation-setting

### Phase 3: expand

Expand by:

- adding adjacent specialties
- extending from clinician guidance into staff orchestration
- connecting more tightly to specialty pharmacy workflows
- making affordability and patient communication more proactive

## Final recommendation

If you want the segment most likely to produce early product-market fit, a technically credible deployment, and strong renewal potential, focus first on **large health systems and IDNs with specialty-heavy ambulatory clinics and centralized medication access teams**, beginning with **rheumatology, gastroenterology, dermatology, and neurology**.

This segment best matches the evidence in the problem statement and research report because it has the highest concentration of medication access friction, the clearest ROI, the strongest implementation readiness, and the most realistic path to repeatable customer success.
